# Non-Specialty Coffee

---

## Why Is Coffee So Bitter in Malaga?

Malaga sits in the heart of Andalusia, which has the strongest torrefacto tradition in Spain. The bitterness has two overlapping causes — one is the beans, one is cultural.

### The Bean Problem: Torrefacto and Mezcla

Torrefacto is a roasting method where sugar is added during the roasting process. It caramelizes on the bean surface, creating a dark, oil-coated bean with a sharp, persistent bitterness that no amount of milk removes. It was introduced in Spain in the 1940s — partly to extend shelf life of cheap green coffee, partly as a wartime sugar-preservation measure. It never left Andalusia.

Pure torrefacto is rare now. What's common is **mezcla** — a blend of natural-roasted and torrefacto beans. Visual tell: two distinct bean colors in the same bag, with greasy dark beans mixed with lighter ones.

Most traditional bars in Malaga use mezcla without disclosing it. The label, if it exists at all, says "mezcla" or "natural/torrefacto" in small print.

### The Cultural Layer: "Intenso" as a Virtue

Even without torrefacto, Malaga bars default to dark-roasted commercial blends. "Fuerte", "intenso", and "extra" are sold as positive attributes. This reflects a genuine regional preference for strong, bitter coffee — but it also masks a supply chain problem: dark roasting hides defects in cheap green coffee, so the preference and the economics reinforce each other.

The result: even when torrefacto isn't present, the coffee is over-roasted, often robusta-heavy, and served through machines that aren't cleaned thoroughly.

### The Dirty Machine Multiplier

Rancid coffee oils accumulate in group heads and portafilters. On already dark, defect-heavy coffee, this amplifies every negative note — adding stale, fermented, almost rotten bitterness on top of the roast bitterness. A dirty machine is a reliable signal that the operator doesn't prioritize what's in the cup.

## Non-Specialty Coffee for Filter

Most commercial coffee is roasted for espresso — dark, to produce crema and withstand milk. This makes it unsuitable for filter brewing, where there's nowhere to hide behind pressure, temperature, or milk.

For filter brewing, you need:
- **Medium roast** — enough development without char
- **No torrefacto** — the caramelized coating is incompatible with filter extraction
- **Reasonable arabica content** — robusta-heavy blends are too harsh and astringent on filter
- **Not stale** — commercial coffee rarely has a roast date, but freshness still matters

The honest commercial tier (Tier 2) can work on filter if the roast is medium and the grind is coarser than for espresso. The expectation is a clean, simple cup — not complex, but not offensive.

## Best Available Non-Specialty

### Portuguese Brands (most accessible Tier 2)

Portugal's coffee culture is built on medium-roast espresso with clean execution. These brands are available in Spanish supermarkets and represent honest commercial coffee:

| Brand | Roast | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Delta** | Medium | Portugal's largest specialty-adjacent brand; clean, consistent |
| **Nicola** | Medium | Similar profile to Delta; widely available |
| **Buondi** | Medium | Slightly darker; decent for espresso, borderline for filter |

These are not specialty. They have no SCA scores, no traceability. But they are roasted without torrefacto, at a medium level, with consistent execution.

### Italian Brands

Italian commercial coffee is dark but without torrefacto. On filter it tends toward bitter and flat, but it's cleaner than mezcla:

| Brand | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Lavazza Qualità Rossa** | Robusta-heavy; standard bar coffee |
| **Lavazza Tierra** | Better arabica content; more suitable for filter |
| **Illy** | All-arabica, consistent; arguably the ceiling of non-specialty commercial |

Illy occupies an unusual position — it's priced above commodity, uses a proprietary pressurized tin to preserve freshness, and sources arabica globally. It doesn't publish SCA scores or farm traceability, but the cup quality is noticeably above standard commercial.

### Spanish Brands to Avoid

Most Spanish supermarket brands use mezcla or dark commercial blends. Brands like Bonka (Nestlé), Marcilla, and Soley are commodity grade and often contain torrefacto. Check the label: "mezcla" or "torrefacto" means avoid.

## Taste Preferences by Region

Understanding the regional context explains why specialty coffee struggles in some markets and thrives in others.

### Andalusia (Malaga, Sevilla, Granada)

The strongest torrefacto tradition in Spain. Dark, bitter, milky coffee is the cultural norm. "Café con leche" is the default — espresso diluted with hot milk, which softens the bitterness but doesn't remove it. Specialty coffee is seen as weak or acidic by the traditional palate. The specialty scene exists but is small and concentrated in tourist and expat areas.

### Catalonia (Barcelona)

The most developed specialty coffee scene in Spain. Barcelona has a critical mass of specialty roasters (Nomad, Right Side, El Magnífico) and informed consumers. The cultural influence of European travel and international population has shifted preferences faster than in the south.

### Basque Country (Bilbao, San Sebastián)

Strong coffee culture with Italian influence. High-quality espresso is expected in bars. Less torrefacto than Andalusia. Receptive to specialty but the bar espresso culture is deeply rooted.

### Galicia (Santiago, Vigo)

Portuguese-influenced. Medium roast, cleaner extraction, less torrefacto. The honest Tier 2 described above is closer to the Galician baseline than the Andalusian one. This is why coffee tastes noticeably better when crossing into Galicia from the south.

### Portugal (Porto, Lisboa, Minho)

The Tier 2 reference point. Medium roast espresso as the cultural standard, no torrefacto, clean machines, consistent technique. Not specialty — but honest. The gap between Portugal and Malaga is the same gap as between Tier 2 and Tier 3.

### France

Dark roast tradition, often with chicory blends in the north. Similar to Spain in preferring strong, bitter coffee. The specialty scene is growing in Paris and Lyon but the baseline is comparable to southern Spain.

### Italy

The espresso origin. Dark roast, robusta blends for crema, no torrefacto. The culture values consistency and technique over origin or traceability. Specialty exists (especially in northern cities) but is culturally marginal. The Italian commercial baseline is higher than Spain's on technique but similar on roast darkness.
