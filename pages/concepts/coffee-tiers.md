# Coffee Tiers: Specialty, Honest Commercial, and Bad

---

## Overview

Not all non-specialty coffee is the same. There are three meaningfully different tiers — and knowing which you're dealing with changes how you respond to it.

In Malaga, the gap between Tier 1 and Tier 3 is wide. Tier 2 is rare locally — you're more likely to find it in Galicia or Portugal than in a Malaga bar.

---

## Tier 1 — Specialty Coffee

**What it is:** SCA-scored (80+), traceable to farm or lot, roasted light to medium to reveal origin character.

**What it feels like:**
- The bag smells immediately when opened — bread-like, fruit, floral, or chocolate depending on origin
- The brew is tea-like in clarity on filter methods
- No bitterness — acidity is bright, not harsh
- Complex but not aggressive
- You can smell the difference before you even brew it

**Reference experience:** A filter coffee at La Hacienda — bread-like aroma, tea-like cup, nothing burnt, complexity that's hard to describe but immediately recognizable as different.

**What to look for on the bag:**
- Roast date (not best-before)
- SCA score or Q Grader evaluation
- Farm or producer name
- Processing method
- Light or filter roast profile

---

## Tier 2 — Honest Commercial Coffee

**What it is:** Below specialty grade (no SCA score, commodity green), but roasted with care — medium roast, clean, no torrefacto, no "intenso" pretension. Competent execution.

**What it feels like:**
- Consistent and predictable
- No burnt bitterness, no harshness
- Less complex than specialty — you won't find fruit notes or brightness
- Drinkable black or with milk
- A clean machine and correct technique matter here more than with specialty

**Where you find it:** Galicia (Spain), Portugal — particularly the café culture around Minho and the Algarve. Portuguese brands like Delta and Nicola represent this tier at their best: medium roast, proper espresso technique, clean equipment.

**What it is NOT:** Dark, "intenso", forte, or strong. Those labels signal the opposite.

---

## Tier 3 — Bad Coffee

Two distinct subtypes, both common in Malaga.

### 3a — Mezcla / Torrefacto

**What it is:** A blend of natural-roasted beans and torrefacto beans (roasted with added sugar, which caramelizes on the surface). Sugar was historically added to extend shelf life and standardize cheap, defective green coffee.

**Visual tells:**
- Two distinct bean colors in the same bag or grind — some beans very dark and greasy, some lighter
- Oily/greasy residue on the surface of the espresso or coffee
- Beans look coated, not just oily from roasting

**Taste:** Bitter regardless of how much milk is added. The bitterness is not roast bitterness — it's a sharp, persistent, structural bitterness from the caramelized sugar. No complexity beneath it.

**Where you find it:** Traditional bars and cafes in Malaga that haven't changed their supplier in decades. Often unlabeled or hidden inside "blend" or "mezcla" bags.

### 3b — Dark Commercial ("Intenso / Forte / Strong")

**What it is:** Over-roasted commercial arabica or robusta blend, no torrefacto, but roasted dark to mask defects in cheap green coffee. Sold under "intenso", "forte", or "strong" branding as if darkness were a virtue.

**Visual tells:**
- Uniformly very dark beans, heavy oil on surface
- No two-tone variation (unlike mezcla)
- "Intenso", "forte", "strong", or "extra" on the packaging

**Taste:** Bitter, flat, no complexity. Carbon and ash notes rather than the chemical torrefacto bitterness. Worse when the machine is not cleaned regularly — rancid coffee oils amplify every negative note.

**Where you find it:** Offices, fast-food chains, most cafes that are not specialty-focused. The default commercial offer in Malaga.

**The dirty machine multiplier:** Both Tier 3 subtypes are significantly worse when served through an uncleaned machine. Coffee oils go rancid and accumulate in group heads and portafilters — this compounds the bitterness and adds stale, fermented off-notes. A dirty machine is a reliable signal that the operator doesn't care about what's in the cup.

---

## The Malaga Gap

In most cities, there's a functional Tier 2 — competent commercial coffee that isn't specialty but isn't offensive. In Malaga, this tier is largely absent. The local default is Tier 3 (mezcla or dark commercial), and the jump to Tier 1 (specialty) is steep with few options in between.

This is why the specialty guide matters here more than in cities with a broader baseline.

---

## Quick Identification

| Signal | Tier 1 | Tier 2 | Tier 3a (mezcla) | Tier 3b (dark commercial) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bean appearance | Light-medium brown, matte | Medium brown, slight sheen | Two colors, greasy dark + lighter | Very dark, heavy oil |
| Smell (bag) | Immediate, complex | Mild, clean | Faint, smoky | Faint or burnt |
| Taste | Complex, no bitterness | Clean, simple | Bitter, persistent | Bitter, flat |
| Milk helps? | Unnecessary | Improves body | Doesn't remove bitterness | Slightly masks |
| Roast date on bag | Yes | Sometimes | No | No |
| Label language | Origin, process, score | Blend, medium roast | Mezcla, natural | Intenso, forte, strong |
